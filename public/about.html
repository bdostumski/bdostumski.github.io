The Story

I am a self-taught software engineer, but my story didn't begin with a computer. It began with a feeling.

The Spark — Arcade Games & A Wooden Shack by the Sea

On a family vacation at the seaside, I wandered into a wooden shack filled with arcade games. I had never seen anything like it. At home, our TVs didn't even have remote controls yet — and here I was, controlling a character on a screen with a joystick. The feeling was electric. Something clicked in that moment that would quietly shape the rest of my life.

When we got back to Blagoevgrad, my cousin and I immediately went looking for a place to play arcade games. We found one, of course. From that point on, our days were split between three places: the arcade, the basketball court, and wherever our curiosity took us.

Nintendo, Computer Garages & Candy Box Dreams

Not long after, my parents bought a Nintendo home video game console. My cousin and I would spend hours on it — or head to the arcade to meet friends, or back to the basketball court.

Then, when I was in 3rd grade, a computer garage opened near our home. Ten computers, about thirty people crammed inside one garage — and it was my first time touching a real computer. Love at first sight. Again.

I started begging my parents to buy me a computer at home. I was so obsessed that I built a computer out of candy boxes — clicking on the cardboard keys, telling my mom:

"One day I will build a computer in a suitcase that I can carry with me everywhere."

Looking back, I was describing a laptop before I even knew the word.

5th Grade — My First Real Computer

In 5th grade, my parents finally got me my first computer. In the beginning, I had to reinstall the operating system myself more times than I can count — but every reinstall taught me something new about how the machine worked.

Until around 7th or 8th grade, I was passionate about games. I associated computers mostly with graphics and had no idea that programming even existed. This passion for the visual side led me, after 7th grade, to apply to the National Humanitarian High School (NHG) in Blagoevgrad, with a fine arts profile.

The Turning Point — Discovering Programming

Towards the end of 8th grade, I met a friend who was learning to program in Visual Basic 6.0. That conversation changed everything. From that moment on, I stepped onto the path of programming — entirely self-taught, driven by curiosity, guided by books.

Life, Business & The Long Detour

In my free time during school, I often helped my father in his grocery store — loading goods, working the cash register, learning the rhythm of honest work.

After graduating from 12th grade, my father and I started a family business focused on construction. I gradually moved away from programming during this time, but I gained something equally valuable — real-world experience across every dimension of running a business:

  • Production & Assembly — Hands-on building and manufacturing
  • Marketing & Sales — Finding customers and closing deals
  • Business Management — Operations, logistics, and planning
  • Negotiations & Contracts — Writing and negotiating business agreements

As a startup entrepreneur, I went through every stage of a business from the ground up.

The Return — WordPress, PHP & Coming Full Circle

Years later, I decided to build a WordPress website for our construction company to present our products. It was a simple idea — but it worked remarkably well. Through that website, we attracted a wave of new customers.

That success reignited something. I decided to build a custom PHP application to track income, expenses, customers, new orders, completed orders, and more. It was my own small business management system — and I was proud of it. The programmer in me had never really left. He was just waiting.

Telerik Academy & The Professional Path

Energized and certain about my direction, I enrolled in university — then dropped out. I enrolled in Telerik Academy, graduated, and committed fully to software engineering.

I would say that the beginning of my professional career as a software engineer started somewhere around 2020 — and it continues to this day.

Philosophy & Tools I Live By 🛠️

I am an Emacs user, a Vim believer, and an Arch Linux devotee.

I use Arch, btw.

Emacs is not just my editor — it is my operating environment. From writing code and managing projects with Org Mode, to taking notes and publishing this very website — Emacs is the lens through which I interact with my digital world. Its infinite extensibility mirrors the self-taught philosophy: if something doesn't exist, you build it yourself.

Vim lives in my muscle memory. Whether I'm editing a config file over SSH or navigating code at the speed of thought with Evil Mode inside Emacs, Vim keybindings are the language my fingers speak.

Arch Linux is my operating system of choice because it refuses to make decisions for you. A minimal base installation, a rolling release model, and the legendary ArchWiki — Arch rewards those who want to understand every layer of their system.
